What is IV15?

IV15 is an intrinsic value estimate built on a single disciplined assumption: a business must be able to compound its free cash flow at 15% per year for 15 years and be worth 15× its final-year cash flow at exit — all discounted back at a 15% hurdle rate. If the stock trades below this value, it clears the bar; if it trades above, you're paying a premium for growth that may never materialise.

IV15 = Σ FCFt / (1.15)t + TerminalValue / (1.15)15

The Tragic Algebra — Why SBC Matters

Companies report Free Cash Flow without deducting Stock-Based Compensation (SBC). SBC is a real cost to shareholders — it dilutes ownership — but because it is a non-cash expense it conveniently disappears from the cash flow statement.

The "Tragic Algebra" corrects this:

True FCF = Reported FCF − Stock-Based Compensation

A company with $10 B in FCF but $4 B in annual SBC has a True FCF of only $6 B. Valuing it on the reported figure overstates intrinsic value by ~67%.

Manual SBC override: Yahoo Finance sometimes fails to report SBC or reports it with the wrong sign. If results look off, find the SBC figure in the company's latest 10-K / annual report (cash flow statement, operating activities section) and enter it manually in millions.

How to interpret Price / IV15

  • < 1.0 — stock trades below intrinsic value at a 15% growth assumption. Potential margin of safety.
  • 1.0 – 1.5 — modest premium; growth expectations are priced in but not extreme.
  • > 1.5 — significant premium; the market is pricing in growth well above 15% per year.

IV15 is not a price target — it is a hurdle test. A ratio above 1 does not mean the stock is a bad investment; it means the implied return at the current price is below 15%/yr under these assumptions. Adjust growth rate and terminal multiple to stress-test your thesis.
